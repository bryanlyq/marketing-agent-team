# Market Opportunity Analysis

## Purpose
Evaluate the size, attractiveness, and strategic feasibility of a market opportunity before committing resources — so growth investment is directed where it can generate the most return.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Evaluating entry into a new market, vertical, or geography
- Assessing the viability of a new product or offer
- Prioritising between competing growth vectors
- Building a business case for board or investor audiences
- Stress-testing a strategic plan against market reality

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Opportunity hypothesis** — What is the specific market or segment being assessed?
2. **Decision being made** — Enter, expand, accelerate, or exit?
3. **Time horizon** — Short-term revenue capture or long-term category play?
4. **Constraints** — Budget, team, technology, or regulatory limitations?
5. **Output audience** — Internal strategy team, board, investors, or GTM team?

---

## Analysis Framework

### 1. Market Sizing — TAM / SAM / SOM

| Level | Definition | How to Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| **TAM** (Total Addressable Market) | Maximum revenue if 100% market share | Top-down: industry reports × ARPU |
| **SAM** (Serviceable Addressable Market) | Portion addressable given GTM model and geography | Bottom-up: ICP count × conversion rate × ARPU |
| **SOM** (Serviceable Obtainable Market) | Realistic capture in 3–5 years | SAM × realistic market share given competitive position |

Use both top-down and bottom-up methods. If they diverge significantly, investigate why.

### 2. Market Attractiveness — Porter's Five Forces

| Force | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| Competitive rivalry | How many players? How differentiated? How price-sensitive is competition? |
| Threat of new entrants | What are the barriers to entry? How easy is it to build or copy? |
| Threat of substitutes | What alternatives do buyers have? Are substitutes improving? |
| Buyer power | How many buyers? How concentrated? Can they dictate terms? |
| Supplier power | Are there critical dependencies? What's the cost of switching suppliers? |

Score each force as low / medium / high threat. Markets with multiple high-threat forces are structurally difficult.

### 3. Category Dynamics — Growth and Timing

Assess:
- **Market growth rate** — Is the category growing, stable, or declining?
- **Stage of maturity** — Emerging, growth, mature, or commoditised?
- **Category tailwinds** — Regulatory shifts, technology changes, demographic trends?
- **Category headwinds** — Substitution risk, channel disruption, price pressure?
- **Timing risk** — Is the window open now, or is it premature / too late?

### 4. Competitive Position

Assess the organisation's ability to win in this market:
- Do we have a differentiated product or capability?
- Do we have existing relationships or brand credibility in this segment?
- What is the cost of customer acquisition relative to LTV?
- Do we have a distribution or channel advantage?

Use a 2×2: Market Attractiveness vs Competitive Strength.

| | Low Competitive Strength | High Competitive Strength |
|---|---|---|
| **High Market Attractiveness** | Build or partner before entering | Priority: invest aggressively |
| **Low Market Attractiveness** | Avoid or exit | Milk or maintain selectively |

### 5. Strategic Fit

Evaluate alignment with existing strategy:
- Does this market serve the existing ICP, or require a new GTM motion?
- Does the product fit without significant modification?
- Does winning here build toward a larger category position?
- What is the opportunity cost of pursuing this vs other growth vectors?

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Define the Opportunity Precisely
Vague opportunities produce vague analyses. Specify: which customer, which problem, which geography, which product configuration.

### Step 2 — Size the Market (Top-Down + Bottom-Up)
Build both models. Present the reconciled estimate with confidence range.

### Step 3 — Assess Structural Attractiveness
Apply Porter's Five Forces. Score and summarise the market's structural dynamics.

### Step 4 — Evaluate Competitive Position
Audit direct and indirect competitors. Map your relative position honestly.

### Step 5 — Assess Strategic Fit and Feasibility
Evaluate internal capability gaps and resource requirements. What would it take to win?

### Step 6 — Score and Recommend
Build a clear recommendation: pursue, test, defer, or avoid — with the conditions under which that recommendation would change.

---

## Output Formats

### Opportunity Brief (Strategic)
- Opportunity definition
- Market size (TAM/SAM/SOM with methodology)
- Structural attractiveness summary
- Competitive position assessment
- Strategic fit evaluation
- Recommendation with conditions

### Market Sizing Model
- Top-down assumptions and sources
- Bottom-up calculation
- Scenario range (conservative / base / optimistic)
- Sensitivity table: what variables most affect the estimate?

### Go-to-Market Readiness Assessment
- Segment fit
- Product fit
- Channel fit
- Team and capability fit
- Timeline and investment required
- Risk factors and mitigation

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **TAM as a confidence signal.** A large TAM does not mean the opportunity is attractive. Accessibility and competitive dynamics matter more.
- **Bottom-up without ICP clarity.** You can't build a bottoms-up model without a defined ICP.
- **Ignoring substitutes.** Buyers always have alternatives — including doing nothing.
- **Optimism bias in SOM.** Market share projections are the most commonly inflated number in any business case.
- **No strategic fit assessment.** A great market is worthless if the business can't win in it.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the opportunity defined with enough specificity to make a real decision?
- [ ] Does the market sizing use both top-down and bottom-up methods?
- [ ] Have structural attractiveness and competitive dynamics been assessed honestly?
- [ ] Is the recommendation explicit — enter, test, defer, or avoid?
- [ ] Are the conditions under which the recommendation changes clearly stated?
