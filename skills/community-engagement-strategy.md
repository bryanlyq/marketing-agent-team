# Community Engagement Strategy

## Purpose
Design community programmes that drive advocacy, retention, and product feedback — by creating spaces where customers, prospects, and practitioners connect with each other and with the brand, rather than just consuming from it.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Customer retention is a strategic priority and relationship depth needs to increase
- Word-of-mouth and referral are under-developed as growth channels
- Building a brand in a category where community and peer learning matter
- Existing users are engaged but disconnected from each other
- Product feedback loops are slow and unstructured

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Objective** — Is this primarily for retention, advocacy, product feedback, acquisition, or all four?
2. **Audience** — Who is the community for? Customers only, or prospects too?
3. **Stage** — Is this a new community or scaling/fixing an existing one?
4. **Platform consideration** — What platform fits the audience's existing behaviour?
5. **Resource commitment** — Who will own and moderate the community? At what time commitment?

---

## Community Types

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Customer community** | Existing customers connect and learn from each other | Retention, advocacy, product feedback |
| **Practitioner community** | Anyone in the field, not just customers | Brand credibility, thought leadership, top-of-funnel |
| **User group** | Product-focused community for power users | Adoption, feature feedback, upsell |
| **Event-based community** | Built around recurring events (webinars, summits) | Episodic engagement; lead generation |
| **Partner / channel community** | For resellers, integrators, or affiliates | Partner enablement and loyalty |

Define the community type before choosing the platform. The objective dictates the design.

---

## Platform Selection

Match the platform to where the audience already spends time:

| Platform | Best For | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Slack** | Practitioners; technical users; active daily engagement | High frequency; real-time; professional |
| **Discord** | Developer, gaming, creator communities | Channels-based; highly active; younger demographic |
| **LinkedIn Groups** | B2B professional communities | Lower engagement frequency; good for awareness |
| **Circle / Mighty Networks** | Customer communities with structured content | Purpose-built for community; monetisation features |
| **Facebook Groups** | Consumer communities; local / regional | Large user base; declining professional engagement |
| **Forum (Discourse)** | Long-form Q&A; technical knowledge base | Searchable; evergreen content; self-serve |
| **In-product community** | High-engagement product users | Contextual; frictionless; data-connected |

Avoid building on a platform the audience doesn't already use. Migration is hard.

## Process

### Step 1 — Define the Community Purpose
Write a clear community purpose statement. This is not for the marketing website — it is the strategic brief that guides all community decisions:

> "This community exists for [audience] to [primary job: learn / connect / influence / get support] — so that [business outcome: they stay longer / refer more / help us build a better product]."

Without a clear purpose, communities become noise — a Slack group where nobody posts.

### Step 2 — Define Success Metrics

| Metric | What It Indicates |
|---|---|
| Active members (posted in last 30 days) | Engagement health |
| Messages per active member | Depth of participation |
| Member-to-member interaction rate | Community vs broadcast ratio |
| New member activation rate | Onboarding effectiveness |
| Content post frequency | Content vitality |
| Referrals attributed to community | Revenue impact |
| Churn rate difference: community vs non-community members | Retention impact |
| NPS of community members vs non-members | Advocacy impact |

Community success metrics are not vanity metrics. They connect community health to business outcomes.

### Step 3 — Design the Community Architecture
Define the structure before launching:
- **Channels or spaces:** What categories of conversation will exist? (e.g., #general, #product-feedback, #help, #introductions, #events)
- **Content formats:** Will there be member posts only, or brand-created content too? (weekly threads, AMAs, resource libraries)
- **Event programme:** What recurring events will anchor the calendar? (monthly Q&A, weekly office hours, annual summit)
- **Recognition programme:** How are top contributors acknowledged? (badges, spotlights, roles)
- **Moderation standards:** What content is acceptable? What is not? How are violations handled?

Keep the structure simple at launch. Over-complicated architectures produce ghost channels.

### Step 4 — Plan the Launch and Early Adoption
The hardest phase of community building is the first 60 days. Low member count creates low perceived value.

Strategies to accelerate early growth:
- **Seed with power users:** Invite your most engaged, vocal customers first. They set the culture.
- **Create a founding member programme:** First 100 or 500 members get exclusive status, access, or recognition
- **Provide immediate value:** Launch with a library of resources, an AMA with a known expert, or an exclusive event
- **Drive the conversation yourself:** In the first 90 days, the community manager should be the most active member
- **Direct internal referrals:** CS teams and account managers introduce community to new customers during onboarding

### Step 5 — Build the Engagement Programme
Community without ongoing programming goes quiet. Define:

**Weekly cadence:**
- Weekly thread prompt (question to spark conversation)
- Member spotlight or welcome post
- Resource share or tip

**Monthly:**
- AMA with expert, customer, or founder
- Product update discussion or feedback session
- Top contributor recognition

**Quarterly / annually:**
- Community summit or major event
- Community impact report ("here's what you've contributed")
- Member survey and programme updates

### Step 6 — Connect Community to Business Systems
Community generates signal that should flow into the business:
- Product feedback → tagged and routed to the product team
- Feature requests → upvoted and tracked in a feedback tool (Canny, Productboard)
- Customer health signals → flagged to CS when community engagement drops
- Referrals → tracked with UTMs and attribution

A community that generates valuable signal but can't surface it to the right team is an underused asset.

## Output Formats

### Community Strategy Document
- Purpose statement
- Community type and platform rationale
- Success metrics and targets
- Architecture (channels, content formats, events)
- Launch plan
- Engagement programme (weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Moderation standards
- Integration with business systems

### Community Health Dashboard
- Active members vs total members
- Engagement rate trend
- Member-to-member vs brand-to-member interaction ratio
- Top contributors
- New member activation rate
- Business metrics (churn differential, referrals)

### Engagement Programme Calendar
Monthly view of:
- Weekly thread prompts
- Events and AMAs
- Spotlights and recognition moments
- Content shares

### Moderation Guidelines
- Community values and code of conduct
- What content is encouraged
- What content is not permitted
- Escalation process
- Tone of moderation responses

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Community as a marketing broadcast channel.** Posting brand content without enabling member conversation produces a newsletter, not a community.
- **Launching too broadly.** A community with 2,000 lurkers and 10 active members is not a community. Seed deeply with the right 50–100 before scaling.
- **No community manager.** Communities without a dedicated human who actively participates go silent within months. Moderation software does not replace human presence.
- **Wrong platform for the audience.** Building a Discord for a CFO community is a mismatch. Know where the audience lives.
- **No connection to business outcomes.** A community that generates advocacy and feedback but has no mechanism to surface insights to the product and revenue teams is a missed opportunity.

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the community purpose statement specific enough to guide design and moderation decisions?
- [ ] Does the platform selection match where the audience already spends time?
- [ ] Are success metrics connected to business outcomes, not just activity?
- [ ] Is there a 90-day launch plan with specific activities to drive early engagement?
- [ ] Is there a named owner with defined time commitment?
