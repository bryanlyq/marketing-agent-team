# Competitive Landscape Review

## Purpose
Audit the competitive environment to identify where the business can win, where it is vulnerable, and what differentiation is sustainable — so strategy is grounded in market reality, not internal assumptions.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Defining or refining positioning
- Preparing for a product launch or market entry
- Building sales battle cards
- Responding to competitive displacement in the pipeline
- Quarterly strategic review
- Investor or board preparation

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Scope** — Direct competitors only, or indirect and substitutes too?
2. **Depth** — Rapid scan or deep-dive per competitor?
3. **Purpose** — Positioning input, sales enablement, product roadmap, or all three?
4. **Competitors to prioritise** — Any specific threats or emerging players to focus on?
5. **Output use** — Internal strategy, sales team, or external-facing document?

---

## Competitor Categories

| Category | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | Same product, same customer, same job | Head-to-head alternatives |
| **Indirect** | Different product, same job | Adjacent solutions solving the same need |
| **Substitutes** | Different approach entirely | Manual process, spreadsheet, doing nothing |
| **Emerging** | Early-stage players with directional threat | Well-funded startups, category disruptors |

Never analyse only direct competitors. Indirect competition and substitutes often drive more lost deals.

## Process

### Step 1 — Build the Competitor List
Sources:
- Lost deal reasons from CRM
- Sales team intelligence ("who else were they evaluating?")
- Review sites: G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, App Store
- Search: category keywords + "alternatives," "vs," "compared to"
- LinkedIn: who's hiring for the same roles?
- Job postings: what technology are they building with?
- Investor announcements and press releases

### Step 2 — Audit Each Competitor Across Dimensions

For each competitor, assess:

**Positioning**
- What category do they occupy?
- What is their stated value proposition?
- Who is their claimed target customer?
- What is their primary differentiator?

**Product**
- Core capabilities and feature set
- Strengths and weaknesses vs your offering
- Recent product launches or roadmap signals (job postings, changelog, release notes)
- Integration depth and ecosystem

**Pricing**
- Model: per-seat, usage-based, flat-rate, freemium, enterprise custom?
- Published price points (where available)
- Free trial or freemium tier?
- Pricing page positioning signals

**Marketing & Messaging**
- Primary channels (paid search, SEO, social, events, PR)
- Messaging themes and tone
- Content strategy and thought leadership territory
- Share of voice in the category

**Sales Motion**
- PLG, sales-led, or hybrid?
- Typical deal size and sales cycle
- SDR/AE ratio signals (from LinkedIn)
- Partnership and channel strategy

**Customer Base**
- Named customers and logos
- Customer review themes (G2, Capterra)
- Common verticals and sizes
- Expansion vs acquisition focus

### Step 3 — Build a Competitive Matrix
Create a side-by-side comparison across key dimensions. Highlight where each competitor is strongest and weakest.

Avoid scoring everything equally. Force-rank competitors on the 2–3 dimensions that matter most to buyers.

### Step 4 — Identify Competitive Gaps and Differentiation Opportunities

Ask:
- Where are competitors clustered? (Opportunity to differentiate by going elsewhere)
- Where are no competitors strong? (Uncontested positioning territory)
- Where are they weakest according to customer reviews? (Attack vector)
- What narrative are they not owning? (Thought leadership and messaging opportunity)

### Step 5 — Assess Your Relative Position
For each evaluated dimension, score your offering honestly:
- Where are you stronger?
- Where are you parity?
- Where are you weaker?

Do not build competitive strategy on self-assessment alone. Validate with win/loss data and customer interviews.

---

## Output Formats

### Competitor Matrix
Table with competitors as columns and dimensions as rows:
- Positioning statement
- Target customer
- Core differentiator
- Pricing model
- Key strengths (max 3)
- Key weaknesses (max 3)
- Primary threat level: High / Medium / Low

### Positioning Map
2×2 or axis-based visual showing where competitors cluster:
- Choose axes that are meaningful to buyers, not just internally convenient
- Plot your current position and target position

### Battle Cards (Sales Enablement)
Per-competitor one-pagers for sales teams:
- Their pitch (how they describe themselves)
- Their strengths (acknowledge honestly)
- Their weaknesses (evidence-backed)
- Win/loss patterns (when do we beat them? When do they beat us?)
- Handling their objections
- Our positioning against them

### Share-of-Voice Report
- Organic search visibility comparison
- Paid search presence and keywords
- Review volume and ratings over time
- Social and PR activity trends

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Only auditing direct competitors.** Substitutes and indirect alternatives account for a significant share of lost deals.
- **Relying on competitor's own marketing.** Cross-reference against customer reviews, job postings, and win/loss data.
- **Battle cards that mock competitors.** If your battle card requires the competitor to be incompetent, it will fail in the field.
- **Static competitive analysis.** Competitive landscapes shift quarterly. Set a refresh cadence.
- **No link to positioning.** Competitive analysis that doesn't change your messaging or GTM motion was a wasted exercise.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Does the competitor list include indirect competitors and substitutes?
- [ ] Is each competitor assessed on product, pricing, positioning, and channel?
- [ ] Is the analysis grounded in external evidence (reviews, job postings, win/loss), not just website copy?
- [ ] Are gaps and differentiation opportunities explicitly identified?
- [ ] Does this analysis change a strategic decision — positioning, messaging, or GTM?
