# Market Research Analysis

## Purpose
Synthesise primary and secondary research into actionable strategic insight that drives business decisions.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Entering a new market or category
- Validating or challenging strategic assumptions
- Identifying whitespace, trends, or competitive threats
- Supporting a go-to-market or positioning project

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business decision** — What decision does this research need to inform?
2. **Scope** — Geographic, segment, category, or product focus?
3. **Existing data** — What research already exists? What gaps need filling?
4. **Timeline** — Is this a rapid scan or a deep-dive?
5. **Output format** — Who is the audience and what do they need to act?

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Frame the Research Questions
Define 3–5 specific questions the research must answer. Avoid vague mandates like "understand the market." Be precise:
- What jobs are customers trying to get done in this category?
- Where is the category growing or contracting?
- What do competitors do better or worse?

### Step 2 — Identify Sources
| Source Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Primary | Customer interviews, surveys, sales call transcripts, NPS verbatims |
| Secondary | Industry reports, analyst coverage, competitor content, job postings, review sites |
| Behavioural | Search trend data, social listening, web traffic tools |
| Commercial | Nielsen, Mintel, Euromonitor, Forrester, Gartner |

Triangulate across at least two source types before drawing conclusions.

### Step 3 — Synthesise, Don't Summarise
For each research question, move from raw findings → patterns → insight → implication:

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| Finding | "63% of respondents said price was a top concern" |
| Pattern | "Price sensitivity is highest among SMB buyers, not enterprise" |
| Insight | "SMBs are price-anchoring on legacy tools, not true WTP ceiling" |
| Implication | "Pricing page needs to reframe value, not compete on cost" |

### Step 4 — Stress-Test Conclusions
Ask for each insight:
- Is this finding consistent across multiple sources?
- Could the data be misleading (sample bias, recency, framing)?
- Does this contradict existing assumptions — and if so, which is more credible?

### Step 5 — Frame for Action
Every insight must close with a strategic implication. Avoid ending on a data point. End on a decision.

---

## Output Formats

### Insight Report
- Executive summary (3–5 bullets: the critical so-whats)
- Market overview (size, growth, key dynamics)
- Customer intelligence (needs, behaviours, language)
- Competitive landscape (positioning, gaps, threats)
- Strategic implications (what this means for the business)
- Recommended next steps

### Strategic Brief (condensed)
- Situation: what the research shows
- Insight: what it means
- Implication: what the business should do differently

### Trend Analysis
- Trend identification and evidence
- Category impact timeline
- Strategic options in response

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Confusing data with insight.** A number is not an insight. An implication is.
- **Over-weighting recency.** The most recent report is not always the most accurate.
- **Confirmation bias.** Research that only finds what the brief expected is not research.
- **Ignoring contradictory data.** Surface tensions — they often reveal the real strategic question.
- **Generic conclusions.** "The market is growing" tells no one anything useful.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Does every insight connect to a business decision?
- [ ] Are conclusions triangulated across multiple sources?
- [ ] Have I challenged, not just confirmed, the starting hypothesis?
- [ ] Is every recommendation specific and actionable?
- [ ] Would a decision-maker read this and know what to do next?
