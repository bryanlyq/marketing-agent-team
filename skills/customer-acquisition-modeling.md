# Customer Acquisition Modeling

## Purpose
Build rigorous models of the economics of customer acquisition — by channel, segment, and cohort — so that marketing investment is allocated to where it generates the best return, and growth targets are built on defensible assumptions.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Setting marketing budgets and channel allocations
- Evaluating whether acquisition economics are sustainable
- Comparing the efficiency of different channels or programmes
- Building a fundraising narrative or board presentation
- Diagnosing why CAC is rising or ROI is declining

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business model** — SaaS, e-commerce, subscription, transactional?
2. **Data access** — Can we segment CAC by channel, cohort, and customer type?
3. **Time period** — What period are we modelling? (Rolling 3 months, 12 months, trailing cohorts)
4. **LTV model** — Is there an existing LTV model to pair with CAC?
5. **Decision** — Is this for channel optimisation, budget allocation, or investor narrative?

---

## Core CAC Concepts

### Blended vs Channel CAC

**Blended CAC**
Total sales and marketing cost ÷ total new customers acquired in the period.

Limitations: masks channel-specific efficiency differences; distorted by channel mix shifts.

**Channel CAC**
(Direct channel cost + attributed sales cost) ÷ customers acquired via that channel.

This is the operational metric for optimisation decisions.

### CAC Components
CAC should include all costs involved in acquiring a customer:

| Category | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **Media spend** | Paid search, paid social, display, sponsorships |
| **Content and creative** | Production costs attributed to acquisition |
| **Marketing team cost** | Salaries and tools, prorated to acquisition activity |
| **Sales team cost** | SDR and AE salaries, commissions, tools |
| **Technology** | MAP, CRM, analytics tools — prorated |

Common mistake: only including ad spend in CAC. This dramatically understates the true cost.

### CAC Payback Period
Time (in months) required to recover the CAC from gross margin generated by the customer:

> **CAC Payback = CAC ÷ (Monthly ARPU × Gross Margin %)**

Benchmarks vary by category:
- Consumer SaaS: 12 months or less
- SMB SaaS: 12–18 months
- Mid-market SaaS: 18–24 months
- Enterprise SaaS: 24–36 months

### LTV:CAC Ratio
> **LTV:CAC = Customer Lifetime Value ÷ CAC**

- **<1:1** — Losing money on every acquisition
- **1:1 – 2:1** — Marginal; likely unsustainable
- **3:1** — Industry baseline for SaaS viability
- **>5:1** — Potentially under-investing; growth may be too slow

The right LTV:CAC target depends on the business model, growth stage, and capital strategy.

## Process

### Step 1 — Build the CAC Model

Identify and categorise all acquisition costs for the period:
- Total marketing spend (excluding retention/expansion)
- Total sales cost (SDR, AE — prorated to new business)
- Tool and technology costs (prorated)

Calculate:
- Blended CAC
- Channel CAC for each material acquisition channel (any channel >10% of MQL volume)

### Step 2 — Build the LTV Model

For each customer cohort, estimate:
- **ARPU** — Average revenue per user per month
- **Gross margin** — Revenue minus direct cost of service
- **Monthly churn rate** — Percentage of customers who cancel each month
- **Average customer lifetime** — 1 ÷ monthly churn rate

> **LTV = (ARPU × Gross Margin %) ÷ Monthly Churn Rate**

Build LTV by segment where behaviour differs significantly. Enterprise and SMB cohorts often have radically different LTV profiles.

### Step 3 — Calculate LTV:CAC by Channel

| Channel | Monthly Leads | CAC | LTV | LTV:CAC | Payback (months) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Organic search | 120 | £450 | £3,800 | 8.4x | 4.2 |
| Paid search | 85 | £820 | £3,600 | 4.4x | 8.1 |
| Paid social | 60 | £1,100 | £2,900 | 2.6x | 13.4 |
| Outbound SDR | 45 | £1,350 | £5,200 | 3.9x | 9.7 |
| Referral | 30 | £280 | £4,400 | 15.7x | 2.4 |

### Step 4 — Analyse Trends Over Time
Plot CAC and LTV:CAC over 12–24 months:
- Is CAC rising, stable, or falling?
- If rising: Is it channel saturation, competition, or mix shift?
- Is LTV:CAC compressing or expanding?
- Which cohorts show the best long-term retention?

### Step 5 — Model Optimisation Scenarios

For each lever, model the CAC or LTV impact:

| Lever | Direction | CAC Impact | LTV Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Increase organic traffic | More inbound | Decrease | Neutral |
| Improve trial-to-paid conversion | More efficient funnel | Decrease | Increase |
| Increase ACV for target segment | Higher deal size | Neutral | Increase |
| Reduce churn by 1% | Better retention | Neutral | Increase |
| Shift budget from social to referral | Channel mix | Decrease | Increase |

### Step 6 — Build Acquisition Budget Recommendation
Using channel CAC data and volume capacity:
- Allocate more budget to high LTV:CAC channels
- Set volume caps where channels are approaching saturation
- Invest in LTV improvement (retention, expansion) if CAC is high relative to LTV

## Output Formats

### CAC Model
Spreadsheet with:
- Cost inputs by category
- New customer volume by channel
- Blended and channel CAC
- Month-over-month trend

### LTV:CAC Dashboard
- LTV:CAC by channel and segment
- CAC payback period
- Revenue generated per £1 of acquisition spend
- Trend chart: 12-month rolling average

### Channel Efficiency Report
- Ranked channel table (by LTV:CAC and payback)
- Budget allocation recommendation
- Volume and quality trade-offs per channel
- Growth constraints by channel

### Acquisition Economics Summary (Board/Investor)
- Blended CAC and LTV:CAC
- CAC payback period
- Year-over-year trend
- Key improvement levers and projected impact

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Excluding sales costs from CAC.** The most common CAC understatement — especially in sales-led motions.
- **Blended CAC as the only metric.** Channel-level CAC is what drives operational decisions.
- **LTV based on aspirational retention.** Use actual cohort data, not the hoped-for churn rate.
- **Ignoring payback period.** A 10:1 LTV:CAC with a 36-month payback has cash flow implications that matter.
- **Optimising CAC in isolation.** Cheapest acquisition is meaningless if those customers churn in month two.
