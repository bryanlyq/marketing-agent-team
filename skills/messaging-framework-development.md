# Messaging Framework Development

## Purpose
Build a hierarchical messaging architecture that communicates the right value to the right audience at every touchpoint — consistently, without sounding generic or interchangeable with competitors.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Launching a new product, brand, or category
- Repositioning after a product pivot or market shift
- Fixing inconsistent messaging across sales, marketing, and product
- Building a campaign that needs a unified narrative
- Onboarding a new agency or content partner

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Audience** — Who does this messaging need to work for? (Segments, personas, buying roles)
2. **Objective** — Awareness, consideration, conversion, or retention?
3. **Competitive context** — What do competitors claim? What territory is unclaimed?
4. **Existing messaging** — What's currently in use? What's working or failing?
5. **Channels** — Where will this messaging appear? (Constraints vary by channel)

---

## Messaging Hierarchy

Build messaging from the top down. Each level informs the next.

```
Brand Message (who we are and why we exist)
    └── Category Message (what problem we solve and for whom)
            └── Product Message (what our product does and why it's better)
                    └── Segment Messages (tailored value for each priority audience)
                            └── Channel Messages (adapted for each format and platform)
```

Never build channel or campaign messages without the brand and category layers beneath them. That's how messaging becomes inconsistent.

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Define the Messaging Inputs
Before writing a single message, gather:
- **Customer language** — Verbatim from interviews, reviews, support tickets, sales calls
- **Jobs-to-be-done** — What are customers actually trying to accomplish?
- **Competitor messaging** — What is already claimed? What's available?
- **Internal POV** — What does the business believe that others don't?
- **Proof points** — What evidence supports each claim?

### Step 2 — Write the Brand Message
The brand message is not a tagline. It is the answer to: "Why does this company exist, and who is it for?"

It should be:
- True (not aspirational fluff)
- Differentiated (not what every competitor also says)
- Relevant (meaningful to the target audience)
- Durable (stable across campaigns and contexts)

Test it: Could your three biggest competitors say the same thing? If yes, rewrite it.

### Step 3 — Define the Category Message
Define what problem the brand solves and why the category matters:
- What is the problem we're solving?
- Why does it matter more than buyers currently recognise?
- What is the cost of not solving it?
- Why now?

Category messaging is particularly important for new products or re-positioned offerings where buyers don't yet understand why they need the solution.

### Step 4 — Build Product Messages
For each core product or capability, define:
- **Headline claim** — The primary benefit in one sentence
- **Proof point** — The specific evidence that backs it up
- **Differentiator** — Why this claim is unique to you, not every competitor

Avoid feature-listing. Lead with outcome, support with feature.

### Step 5 — Adapt for Segments
For each priority segment, adjust:
- Which benefit to lead with (different jobs = different priorities)
- Which proof points resonate most
- Which language and framing fits (buyer's vocabulary, not yours)
- Which objections to pre-empt

### Step 6 — Define Tone and Voice Guardrails
The messaging framework needs execution guardrails:
- What tone is appropriate? (Confident, warm, technical, provocative?)
- What words or phrases to use consistently
- What words or phrases to avoid (jargon, competitor-owned terms, overused claims)
- What the brand should never sound like

---

## Output Formats

### Messaging House
A single-page architecture showing:
- Brand promise (roof)
- Pillars (3–4 core messages)
- Proof points per pillar (supporting evidence)
- RTBs (reasons to believe: data, case studies, product features)
- Tone and voice summary (foundation)

### Value Proposition Statement
For each segment or use case:
- For [audience]
- Who [have this problem or need]
- [Brand] is a [category]
- That [primary benefit]
- Unlike [alternative]
- We [key differentiator]

### Message Matrix
Table mapping:
- Audience (column)
- Message pillar (row)
- Tailored headline
- Supporting proof points
- Tone adjustments

### Copy Pillars Document
3–5 named themes that all content and campaigns should express:
- Pillar name
- What it means
- What it does not mean
- Example headline
- Content types that express it

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Generic messages.** "We help businesses grow" belongs to no one and means nothing.
- **Feature-led messaging.** Buyers don't buy features. They buy outcomes.
- **Messaging untested against customer language.** If your audience wouldn't use these words to describe the problem, rethink the messaging.
- **No proof points.** A claim without evidence is marketing noise.
- **One message for all audiences.** Segment-specific adaptation is not optional.
- **Framework as final deliverable.** The framework is the input to execution, not the output to the market.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Could a competitor say the same thing? If yes, rewrite.
- [ ] Is each message grounded in customer language and validated proof points?
- [ ] Does the framework have a hierarchy — brand → category → product → segment?
- [ ] Are tone and voice guardrails included?
- [ ] Can a copywriter use this to brief an ad, a landing page, and a sales deck without asking follow-up questions?
