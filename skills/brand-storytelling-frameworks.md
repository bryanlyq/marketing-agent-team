# Brand Storytelling Frameworks

## Purpose
Build narrative architectures that give the brand a consistent, compelling story to tell — across founder communication, marketing, sales, and employee contexts — so the brand is remembered and chosen for reasons beyond product features.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Brand lacks a coherent story and feels different depending on who tells it
- Preparing a founder narrative for fundraising or media
- Sales team pitches product features without a connecting story
- Building a new brand or repositioning an existing one
- Onboarding new employees, agencies, or partners who need the brand story

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Audience** — Who is the story primarily for? (Customers, investors, employees, media?)
2. **Objective** — What should the audience feel, believe, or do after hearing this story?
3. **Raw material** — What is the genuine origin, belief, or tension the story is built from?
4. **Current story** — What story does the brand currently tell, and why isn't it working?
5. **Constraints** — Legal, competitive, or cultural limits on what can be claimed?

---

## Why Stories Work

Stories are the brain's preferred format for receiving and retaining information. A story:
- Makes abstract claims concrete and memorable
- Creates emotional connection that logic alone cannot
- Provides a frame through which all subsequent information is filtered
- Travels — people retell stories; they don't retell feature lists

The brand that controls its own story controls how it is perceived. The brand without a story gets one assigned by the market — usually a commodity label.

## Core Story Frameworks

### 1. The Origin Story
**Purpose:** Establish authenticity and mission; answer "why does this exist?"

Structure:
- **The world as it was** — The problem or gap that existed before
- **The moment of realisation** — What the founder or team saw that others didn't
- **The decision to act** — Why they were compelled to do something about it
- **The mission forward** — What they're building and why it matters

Origin stories work when they are specific and genuine. Vague origin stories ("we wanted to make things better") signal inauthenticity. The most powerful origin stories come from personal frustration with the problem.

### 2. The Customer Story
**Purpose:** Create peer identification; demonstrate real-world value

Structure:
- **Before** — Who was the customer and what was their world like before? (Name the pain specifically)
- **Catalyst** — What triggered them to seek a solution?
- **The decision** — Why did they choose this brand over alternatives?
- **After** — What is their world like now? (Name the outcome specifically, in numbers where possible)
- **The implication** — What does this mean for others like them?

The most common mistake in customer stories: featuring the brand as the hero. The customer is the hero. The brand is the guide.

### 3. The Category Story
**Purpose:** Frame the problem in a way that makes the brand's solution inevitable

Structure:
- **The old way** — How the problem has been addressed historically
- **Why the old way is broken** — The specific, named failure mode
- **The new way** — A different approach made possible by a shift in technology, behaviour, or understanding
- **Why this matters now** — What has changed to make this the right moment
- **The invitation** — An implicit or explicit call to make the change

Category stories are particularly powerful for new products or repositioned ones. They don't sell the product directly — they make the audience want the change the product delivers.

### 4. The Impact Story
**Purpose:** Connect the brand to a larger purpose; relevant for B2C, mission-driven brands, ESG narrative

Structure:
- **The problem in the world** — A genuine, named problem the brand cares about
- **The connection** — How the brand's work addresses this problem
- **The evidence** — Specific, credible proof of impact (not aspirational claims)
- **The collective** — How customers, employees, or partners participate in the impact
- **The horizon** — Where this leads if it succeeds

Impact stories only work if the connection to the core business is genuine. Forced purpose-washing creates cynicism.

### 5. The Contrast Story
**Purpose:** Create clear positioning against the status quo or direct competitors

Structure:
- **Everyone else believes X**
- **We believe Y** (the contrarian position)
- **Here's why X is wrong** (evidence and argument)
- **Here's what Y looks like in practice** (specifics)
- **The choice** (implicit invitation to side with the brand)

Contrast stories require confidence. They work when the brand genuinely holds a different view — not when the contrast is manufactured for positioning purposes.

## The Story Architecture

For any brand, the full narrative architecture maps stories across contexts:

| Context | Story Type | Primary Audience | Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fundraising / investor | Origin + Category | Investors, board | 5–10 min |
| Sales pitch | Customer + Contrast | Buyers | 2–3 min |
| Marketing / advertising | Customer + Category | Prospects | 30s–2 min |
| Press / media | Origin + Impact | Journalists, public | 3–5 min |
| Employee onboarding | Origin + Impact | Team | 10–15 min |
| Conference keynote | Category + Contrast | Peers, industry | 20–45 min |

The same core story adapts across these contexts. The underlying truth stays constant; the emphasis and detail shift.

## Process

### Step 1 — Mine the Raw Material
Before writing any story, extract the genuine material:
- Why does the business exist, in its founders' own words? (Not the polished version — the real one)
- What did the founding team see that others didn't?
- What problem have they personally experienced?
- What do customers say about how their world changed?
- What does the business believe about the category that others won't say?

### Step 2 — Identify the Core Tension
Every good story has a tension — a gap between the world as it is and the world as it should be. Name it clearly.

The brand's story lives in that tension. The product is the resolution.

### Step 3 — Choose the Primary Story Framework
Select the framework most relevant to the current need. Start with one story well-told before building the full architecture.

### Step 4 — Write the Story
Draft the story in plain language. Avoid:
- Jargon and business speak
- Vague superlatives ("world-class," "revolutionary," "best-in-class")
- Passive constructions that remove the human
- Claims without specifics

Use: Names, numbers, direct quotes, sensory detail, specific moments.

### Step 5 — Test the Story
Before distributing:
- Does it feel genuine, or does it feel constructed?
- Can the people who will tell it tell it naturally, in their own words?
- Does it create an emotional response as well as an intellectual one?
- Is it specific enough to be remembered and retold?

Test with 5 people who match the intended audience. Ask what they remember 24 hours later.

### Step 6 — Build the Narrative Guide
Turn the story into a guide that enables consistent telling across the organisation:
- The story in full (reference version)
- The story in 60 seconds (elevator version)
- The story in one sentence (headline version)
- Key phrases and language that should be used
- Language and framings that should be avoided
- Common questions and how to answer them within the story

---

## Output Formats

### Brand Narrative Document
- Core story (origin, category, or customer) in full
- Narrative architecture: story by context
- Story guide with 60-second and one-sentence versions
- Language do's and don'ts

### Storytelling Guide
For all people who tell the brand story:
- The story framework and why it works
- The full reference story
- Adapted versions by context
- Practice questions and sample responses

### Content Brief: Story-Driven
For content that carries the brand story:
- Which story framework applies
- The specific audience and their emotional starting point
- The key tension and resolution
- Proof points to include
- The feeling the audience should leave with

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Brand as hero.** The customer is the hero. The brand is the guide that helps them succeed.
- **Stories built on aspiration, not truth.** A fabricated or exaggerated origin story collapses the moment anyone looks closely.
- **No tension.** A story without a problem to solve is a press release.
- **Different story in every channel.** Inconsistent narrative suggests no genuine story. Unify, then adapt.
- **Story that is only for marketing.** If the sales team, CS team, and founder all tell a different version, there is no brand story — only marketing copy.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the customer (not the brand) the hero of the story?
- [ ] Does the story have a genuine tension — a gap between the world as it is and should be?
- [ ] Is the story grounded in specific detail — names, numbers, moments — not vague claims?
- [ ] Can it be told naturally in a 60-second version and still be compelling?
- [ ] Does the narrative guide enable consistent telling across marketing, sales, and leadership?
