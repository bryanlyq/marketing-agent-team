# Retention Strategy Planning

## Purpose
Design programmes that reduce churn, increase lifetime value, and build a customer base that grows through loyalty and advocacy — because acquiring a new customer costs 5–7x more than retaining an existing one.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Churn rate is above category benchmarks
- Net revenue retention (NRR) is below 100% (the base is shrinking)
- No structured onboarding or lifecycle programme exists
- Customer satisfaction (NPS, CSAT) is declining
- Growth depends on acquisition because the base doesn't sustain itself

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Current churn rate** — Gross and net revenue retention by segment and cohort
2. **Churn signals** — What behavioural or survey data exists about why customers leave?
3. **Existing retention programmes** — What onboarding, engagement, and win-back activities exist?
4. **Customer success structure** — CS team capacity, tooling, and current motion
5. **Business model** — Subscription, usage-based, contract, transactional?

---

## Retention Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Target (SaaS B2B) |
|---|---|---|
| Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) | (MRR retained before expansion) ÷ Starting MRR | >85–90% |
| Net Revenue Retention (NRR) | (MRR after churn + expansion) ÷ Starting MRR | >110% |
| Logo Churn Rate | Customers lost ÷ Customers at start of period | <5% annually (enterprise) |
| Monthly Churn Rate | Customers lost in month ÷ Customers at month start | <1% (SMB SaaS) |
| Time to First Value | Days from sign-up to first meaningful outcome | Minimise; define per product |
| Product Adoption Rate | % customers using core features within 30 days | Define per product |

NRR above 100% means the business grows even without acquiring a single new customer. It is the single most important retention metric for SaaS.

---

## Retention Architecture

Structure the programme across four lifecycle phases:

```
ONBOARDING → ENGAGEMENT → RENEWAL → WIN-BACK
```

| Phase | Goal | Primary Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Onboarding** | Reach first value fast | Early churn (0–90 days) |
| **Engagement** | Build usage habits and expand adoption | Silent churn — customer stops using without cancelling |
| **Renewal** | Retain at contract renewal point | Contractual churn (annual review) |
| **Win-back** | Reactivate churned or at-risk customers | Permanent loss |

## Process

### Step 1 — Diagnose Current Churn
Analyse churn data to identify:
- **When** — At what point in the lifecycle does churn most commonly occur? (First 30 days? At renewal? After a trigger event?)
- **Who** — Which segments, cohorts, or account types churn most?
- **Why** — Exit survey responses, support tickets, sales loss reasons, win-back call notes

Common churn causes:
| Cause | Category | Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Never adopted the product | Onboarding failure | Low feature adoption in first 30 days |
| Did not achieve promised outcome | Value gap | Low NPS; support tickets |
| Price no longer justified | Perceived value / budget | Pricing objection at renewal |
| Champion left | Relationship risk | Key contact change at account |
| Competitor | Market loss | Closed-lost reason codes |
| Out of business / acquired | External | Account status change |

Different causes require different retention interventions.

### Step 2 — Define Leading Churn Indicators
Identify the behaviours that predict churn before it happens:
- Usage decline (sessions, feature usage, API calls — depending on product)
- Support escalations or unresolved tickets
- NPS score drop
- Missed QBRs or disengaged stakeholders
- Champion departure (LinkedIn signals)
- Invoice payment delays

These signals should trigger proactive interventions — not wait for the customer to request cancellation.

### Step 3 — Design the Onboarding Programme
Onboarding is the highest-leverage retention investment. Customers who fail to activate in the first 30–60 days rarely become long-term customers.

Define:
- **Time to First Value (TTFV)** — The milestone that marks successful onboarding
- **Onboarding checklist** — Steps required to reach TTFV
- **Activation milestones** — Product usage checkpoints at Day 3, Day 14, Day 30
- **Intervention triggers** — What prompts an email, in-app message, or CS outreach at each point?
- **Welcome journey** — Sequence of emails, in-app tours, and check-in calls

### Step 4 — Design the Engagement Programme
After onboarding, maintain engagement and expand usage:
- **Adoption campaigns** — Prompt customers to use features they haven't tried
- **Health scoring** — Automated flag when engagement drops below threshold
- **QBR programme** — Regular business reviews for key accounts
- **Community and education** — Webinars, user groups, knowledge base content
- **Expansion signals** — Identify customers near usage limits or with unexplored use cases

### Step 5 — Design the Renewal Programme
Prepare for contract renewals before they arrive:
- Start renewal conversations 90 days before renewal date
- Build a renewal health score (usage + NPS + expansion + support history)
- Define playbooks for green (expand), yellow (protect), red (save) accounts
- Equip CS teams with case studies, ROI data, and competitive comparison tools

### Step 6 — Design the Win-Back Programme
For churned customers:
- Define win-back eligibility (not every churned customer is worth pursuing)
- Diagnose the churn reason before reaching out
- Build a targeted win-back sequence tailored to the churn cause
- Offer a specific re-entry incentive (not a generic discount)
- Measure win-back rate and time-to-win-back

## Output Formats

### Retention Strategy Document
- Churn diagnosis (when, who, why)
- Leading indicator framework
- Programme architecture across four phases
- Intervention playbooks per churn signal
- Measurement framework and KPIs

### Churn Risk Framework
| Health Score | Criteria | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Green (80–100) | High usage, NPS promoter, expanding | Upsell and advocacy activation |
| Yellow (50–79) | Declining usage or passive NPS | Proactive CS check-in; adoption campaign |
| Red (<50) | Low usage, detractor NPS, missed QBR | Save play: escalation, exec involvement |

### Lifecycle Programme Map
Timeline showing:
- Day 0: Welcome email + onboarding kickoff
- Day 3: Activation check + first value prompt
- Day 14: Adoption milestone check; intervention if below threshold
- Day 30: Onboarding health review; NPS pulse
- Day 60: Feature adoption campaign
- Day 90: QBR for key accounts; health score review
- 90 days before renewal: Renewal campaign initiation

### Win-Back Playbook
- Target criteria for win-back eligibility
- Outreach sequence by churn reason
- Offer structure
- Re-onboarding plan
- Success metrics

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Treating all churn the same.** Price-sensitivity churn, product-fit churn, and champion-departure churn need different interventions.
- **Retention without activation.** If customers don't reach first value, no retention programme rescues them.
- **Health scores based on activity, not outcomes.** A customer who logs in daily but hasn't achieved their goal is still at risk.
- **Win-back with no diagnosis.** Reaching out to churned customers without understanding why they left produces low conversion and feels tone-deaf.
- **Retention as CS-only responsibility.** Retention is influenced by product (does it deliver value?), marketing (does content reinforce value?), and CS (does the team activate and protect accounts?).

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is churn diagnosed by cause, not just rate?
- [ ] Are leading indicators defined and tracked before churn occurs?
- [ ] Does the programme address all four phases: onboarding, engagement, renewal, win-back?
- [ ] Are intervention triggers defined for each churn risk signal?
- [ ] Is NRR (not just gross churn) being tracked as the primary retention metric?
