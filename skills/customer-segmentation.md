# Customer Segmentation

## Purpose
Define, prioritise, and activate meaningful customer segments to focus marketing effort, sharpen messaging, and allocate budget with precision.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Launching into a new market with an undefined audience
- Rethinking who the business should prioritise
- Building a personalisation or lifecycle marketing programme
- Identifying underserved or over-served segments

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business objective** — Is this to find the highest-value segment, define messaging audiences, or personalise campaigns?
2. **Data availability** — What CRM, survey, or behavioural data exists?
3. **Segment use case** — Will segments be used for targeting, messaging, product, or all three?
4. **Granularity required** — How many segments can the business realistically activate?
5. **Decision deadline** — Is this for an immediate campaign or a longer-term strategic shift?

---

## Segmentation Lenses

Use at least two lenses in combination. Single-variable segmentation is almost always too blunt.

| Lens | Variables | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Demographic | Age, gender, income, education, geography | Consumer markets, media targeting |
| Firmographic | Industry, company size, revenue, growth stage, location | B2B targeting and ICP definition |
| Behavioural | Purchase frequency, recency, feature usage, channel preference | Lifecycle marketing, retention |
| Psychographic | Values, motivations, attitudes, lifestyle, risk tolerance | Brand and messaging strategy |
| Needs-based | Jobs-to-be-done, pain points, desired outcomes | Positioning, product, messaging |
| RFM | Recency, Frequency, Monetary value | E-commerce, CRM prioritisation |

## Process

### Step 1 — Define the Segmentation Purpose
A segment built for brand messaging is different from one built for paid targeting. Clarify the use case before segmenting.

### Step 2 — Gather and Audit Data
- First-party: CRM records, product usage data, transaction history
- Survey data: Jobs-to-be-done, preference, NPS by segment
- Third-party: Market research, panel data

Assess completeness and recency. Flag data gaps that will limit confidence.

### Step 3 — Apply Segmentation Logic
Choose the dominant lens based on the use case, then layer secondary lenses:
- Start with **needs-based or behavioural** for strategic segmentation
- Use **demographic or firmographic** for tactical targeting and list-building
- Use **RFM** for CRM and lifecycle activation

### Step 4 — Prioritise Segments
Evaluate each candidate segment against:

| Criterion | Description |
|---|---|
| Revenue potential | Size × willingness to pay × conversion likelihood |
| Accessibility | Can we reach them efficiently via available channels? |
| Fit | Do we solve a real, urgent problem for this segment? |
| Competitive intensity | Are we competing with entrenched alternatives? |
| Strategic alignment | Does winning here advance longer-term goals? |

Score each segment. Avoid spreading effort equally across all segments.

### Step 5 — Define Segment Profiles
For each prioritised segment, document:
- Who they are (descriptive characteristics)
- What they need (jobs, pains, gains)
- How they buy (channels, triggers, barriers)
- What message resonates (language, proof points)
- Where to reach them (channels, platforms, media)

### Step 6 — Link to Downstream Strategy
Segmentation has no value unless it changes decisions. For each segment, define:
- Channel and media strategy
- Messaging and value proposition
- Offer and product configuration
- Sales approach and qualification criteria

---

## Output Formats

### Segmentation Framework
- Lens selection rationale
- Segment definitions (2–5 named segments)
- Prioritisation matrix with scoring
- Segment profiles (1 page per segment)
- Activation implications per segment

### Persona Matrix (Segmentation-Derived)
- Segment name and label
- Core characteristics
- Jobs-to-be-done
- Key barriers to purchase
- Preferred channels and content formats
- Representative quote

### TAM/SAM/SOM Sizing by Segment
- Total addressable market per segment
- Serviceable addressable market given go-to-market fit
- Realistic capture estimate given competitive position

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Segments that can't be activated.** If you can't target or message to a segment, it's a persona, not a segment.
- **Too many segments.** If you can't resource activation for each one, consolidate.
- **Demographic-only segmentation.** Age and gender rarely drive the most useful strategic decisions.
- **Segments without prioritisation.** All segments are not equal. Rank them.
- **Forgetting the "so what."** Every segment definition must imply a different marketing action.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is each segment meaningfully different from the others?
- [ ] Can each segment be reached and messaged to distinctly?
- [ ] Are segments ranked by strategic priority, not just size?
- [ ] Does each segment profile connect to a specific channel and message recommendation?
- [ ] Could a campaign manager use this to brief an agency tomorrow?
