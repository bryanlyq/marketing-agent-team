# Pricing Strategy Analysis

## Purpose
Evaluate and recommend the pricing model, architecture, and price points that maximise revenue, support positioning, and align with customer value perception — not just cost recovery.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Launching a new product or entering a new market
- Declining conversion rates or excessive discounting
- Repositioning upmarket or downmarket
- Competitive pricing pressure requiring a strategic response
- Freemium / trial models that aren't converting to paid

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business objective** — Revenue maximisation, market share, or margin improvement?
2. **Current pricing** — What model, price points, and discount structure exists?
3. **Customer data** — Is there willingness-to-pay (WTP) data or pricing survey data?
4. **Competitive context** — How are competitors priced and structured?
5. **Constraints** — Margin floor, sales team capacity, contract commitments?

---

## Pricing Model Options

Select the model that fits the value delivery mechanism and buying context:

| Model | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Value-based** | Price set to capture a share of the value delivered to the customer | High-differentiation products; clear ROI |
| **Cost-plus** | Price = cost + margin | Commodities; low differentiation; regulated markets |
| **Competitive** | Price set relative to market alternatives | High-substitution markets; positioning statement |
| **Freemium** | Core product free; premium features paid | High-volume, low-touch acquisition; PLG |
| **Usage-based** | Price scales with consumption | SaaS with variable usage; fairness-sensitive buyers |
| **Per-seat** | Fixed price per user | B2B SaaS with defined user roles |
| **Tiered flat-rate** | Multiple bundles at different price points | Clear buyer segments with different needs |
| **Dynamic** | Price varies by demand, time, or buyer segment | Travel, events, advertising inventory |

Never choose a model because it's "standard for the category." Choose based on where value is delivered and how buyers perceive fairness.

## Pricing Architecture Principles

### Price-to-Value Alignment
The price a customer pays should feel proportional to the value they receive. Misalignment creates churn (overpriced), leaves money on the table (underpriced), or signals the wrong brand position.

### Price Anchoring
- Present higher-priced options first or in context to make the target price feel reasonable
- A premium tier makes the core tier look like a bargain
- Decoy pricing: a mid-tier option that makes the high-tier look like better value

### Price Signalling
Price communicates positioning. Pricing 40% below market signals a commodity, not value. Price is part of brand strategy.

### Willingness-to-Pay (WTP)
WTP varies by:
- Segment (enterprise buyers have higher WTP than SMB)
- Use case (mission-critical vs nice-to-have)
- Time (urgency increases WTP)
- Competitive alternatives (fewer substitutes = higher WTP)

## Process

### Step 1 — Audit Current Pricing Performance
- What is the average deal value by segment, channel, and product?
- What is the discounting rate? Who discounts most and why?
- Where does pricing come up as an objection in sales calls?
- What is the conversion rate by pricing tier?
- Are there segments where pricing friction is highest?

### Step 2 — Assess Willingness-to-Pay
Methods:
- **Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter** — Survey asking four pricing questions to identify acceptable, optimal, and expensive thresholds
- **Gabor-Granger** — Test different price points to identify demand curves
- **Conjoint analysis** — Evaluate trade-offs between features and price
- **Sales call analysis** — When does price close deals vs kill them?
- **Win/loss data** — How often does price cause a loss?

### Step 3 — Map Price to Value Delivered

For each customer segment, document:
- What outcome does the customer achieve?
- What is the quantifiable ROI or cost saving?
- What would they pay for that outcome if your product didn't exist (alternative cost)?

Your price should capture a defensible share of the value delivered — typically 10–30% of the quantifiable value.

### Step 4 — Evaluate Competitive Pricing Context
- Plot competitors on a price vs value matrix
- Identify where positioning gaps exist (e.g., a premium niche with no good option)
- Identify where commoditisation risk is highest
- Determine whether you should price at parity, premium, or discount — and why

### Step 5 — Model Pricing Scenarios

Build three scenarios:

| Scenario | Description | Revenue Impact | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Maintain** | Keep current model; optimise presentation | Low | Low |
| **Adjust** | Modify price points or tiers within current model | Medium | Medium |
| **Restructure** | Change the fundamental pricing model | High | High |

Model the revenue, conversion, and churn impact of each scenario before recommending.

### Step 6 — Recommend with Rationale
Present a clear recommendation with:
- Proposed model and price points
- Justification from WTP, competitive, and value-delivery data
- Transition plan (if changing an existing model)
- Test plan to validate before full rollout

---

## Output Formats

### Pricing Strategy Brief
- Situation (current model, pain points)
- Analysis summary (WTP, competitive, value analysis)
- Recommended model and architecture
- Price points by tier/segment
- Discount policy
- Transition or test plan

### Competitive Pricing Audit
Table showing:
- Competitor
- Model type
- Entry price / starting price
- Key inclusions at each tier
- Positioning signal

### Model Comparison Matrix
Side-by-side of 2–3 pricing model options:
- Revenue potential
- Complexity to implement
- Buyer perception risk
- Strategic fit

### Pricing Page Recommendation
- Recommended tier names and structure
- What each tier includes
- Suggested price points
- Recommended "featured" tier
- Discount and annual commitment mechanics

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Cost-plus as default.** Pricing from cost tells you your floor, not your optimal price.
- **Matching competitors without analysis.** Competitor prices reflect their strategy, not yours.
- **Ignoring WTP by segment.** Enterprise and SMB often have radically different WTP for the same product.
- **Discounting as a fix for a positioning problem.** If buyers don't see the value, discounting won't solve it — repositioning will.
- **Freemium without a conversion path.** A free tier with no clear reason to upgrade is just cost.

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the recommended pricing model aligned to where and how value is delivered?
- [ ] Is there WTP data — not just competitive benchmarking — informing the price points?
- [ ] Are price points differentiated by segment where WTP differs meaningfully?
- [ ] Is there a discount policy that prevents sales from eroding margin?
- [ ] Is there a test plan before full rollout of a new model?
