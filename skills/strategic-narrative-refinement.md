# Strategic Narrative Refinement

## Purpose
Stress-test the company's strategic narrative against market reality and competitive positioning, align narrative across investor, customer, media, and employee communications, and identify narrative gaps, inconsistencies, and credibility risks.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Preparing for a fundraising round, IPO, or acquisition
- After a pivot, major product change, or rebranding
- When market positioning is unclear or inconsistent across channels
- When sales is struggling to articulate value or close enterprise deals
- Periodic narrative audit as market context evolves

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business objective** — Fundraising, repositioning, alignment, or competitive response?
2. **Audience scope** — Which audiences must the narrative serve: investors, customers, media, employees?
3. **Current state** — What is the current narrative? Where does it live (deck, website, sales materials)?
4. **Market context** — What has changed in the market or competitive landscape that requires narrative evolution?
5. **Output format** — Narrative architecture document, messaging alignment audit, or communication guide?

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Document the Current Narrative
Collect all existing narrative artefacts:
- Investor pitch deck
- Website hero and about page
- Sales deck and battle cards
- CEO / executive public statements and talks
- Product marketing positioning documents
- Press releases and media statements

Identify: What story is the business currently telling? Is it consistent across these sources?

### Step 2 — Stress-Test the Narrative Against Four Tests

**Test 1 — Is it true?**
- Can every claim be substantiated with evidence?
- Are there claims that could be challenged by a journalist, investor, or competitor?
- Are there facts the narrative avoids or misrepresents?

**Test 2 — Is it relevant?**
- Does it address the problems or ambitions of each target audience?
- Is it framed in the customer's language or the company's language?
- Would a prospect read this and immediately understand why it matters to them?

**Test 3 — Is it differentiated?**
- Could a competitor make the same claims?
- What specifically makes this business, product, or approach different?
- Does the narrative claim a category position no one else owns?

**Test 4 — Is it coherent?**
- Does the narrative hold together logically from problem → insight → solution → proof?
- Is there a consistent through-line from why the company exists to what it does?
- Are there internal contradictions between different parts of the narrative?

### Step 3 — Assess Cross-Audience Alignment
The same core narrative must translate appropriately for different audiences:

| Audience | What They Need from the Narrative | Gap Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Investors | Why this is a big market and why this team wins | — |
| Customers (ICP) | Why this solves their specific problem better than alternatives | — |
| Media / analysts | Why this is interesting, new, or important | — |
| Employees | Why this work matters and why this company will win | — |
| Partners | Why this is a compelling relationship for shared growth | — |

The core story should be consistent; the emphasis and proof points should flex by audience.

### Step 4 — Identify Credibility Risks
Credibility risks undermine the narrative even when it is technically true:

- **Overclaiming:** Statements that invite challenge ("the only platform that…", "the market leader in…")
- **Generic differentiation:** Claims that sound distinctive but describe the whole category ("easy to use", "powerful")
- **Proof gap:** Claims with no supporting evidence, case studies, or data
- **Narrative lag:** Story that describes what the company was, not what it is now
- **Inconsistency:** Different stories told by the CEO, sales team, and product marketing

### Step 5 — Rebuild the Narrative Architecture
Structure the refined narrative as a hierarchy:

**Level 1 — The Origin Story:** Why does this company exist? What problem did the founders observe that others ignored?

**Level 2 — The Market Insight:** What is true about the world (or changing in the world) that makes this the right time for this solution?

**Level 3 — The Category Position:** What category does the company lead or define? What is the distinctive point of view?

**Level 4 — The Solution Story:** How does the product or service deliver on the promise? What is the mechanism of value?

**Level 5 — The Proof:** Customer outcomes, growth metrics, awards, analyst recognition — evidence that the story is real.

### Step 6 — Build Alignment Across Communications
Define which version of the narrative is the master source of truth, and how each communication channel draws from it:

- Investor narrative → Pitch deck, investor updates, board presentations
- Customer narrative → Website, sales deck, case studies, product marketing
- Media narrative → Press releases, spokesperson talking points, analyst briefings
- Employee narrative → All-hands presentations, onboarding materials, values documents

---

## Output Formats

### Narrative Architecture Document
- Current narrative audit findings
- Stress test results (true, relevant, differentiated, coherent)
- Credibility risks identified
- Refined narrative hierarchy (5 levels)
- Audience-specific emphasis guide

### Messaging Alignment Audit
- Channel-by-channel review of current messaging
- Inconsistencies and gaps identified
- Recommended alignment changes
- Master messaging hierarchy

### Communication Guide
- Core narrative (the definitive version)
- Audience adaptations
- Proof points and evidence library
- Language to use and avoid
- Talking points for spokespeople

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Confusing messaging with strategy.** A better tagline is not a better narrative. The story must reflect genuine strategic differentiation.
- **Writing for internal approval rather than external audience.** The best-sounding narrative internally is often the least compelling externally.
- **Overclaiming uniqueness.** "The only..." and "the first..." invite challenge. Use them only when unambiguously true and verifiable.
- **Ignoring employee alignment.** A narrative that employees don't understand or believe will never survive contact with customers.
- **Treating narrative as static.** Markets change, competition evolves, products develop — revisit the narrative at least annually.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the narrative stress-tested against all four tests (true, relevant, differentiated, coherent)?
- [ ] Are credibility risks identified and addressed?
- [ ] Does the refined narrative serve all key audiences, with appropriate emphasis adjustments?
- [ ] Is there a master narrative source of truth with clear channel derivations?
- [ ] Are proof points documented for every major claim?
