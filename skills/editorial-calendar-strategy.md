# Editorial Calendar Strategy

## Purpose
Build a structured 90-day and annual editorial plan that connects content publishing to business priorities — so content output is intentional, consistent, and connected to the campaigns and objectives that matter.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Content is published reactively with no forward planning
- There is no visibility into what's coming or who owns it
- Campaign content and organic content are not coordinated
- Team is producing volume without strategic alignment
- Scaling a content programme and needing operational structure

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business calendar** — What are the key business milestones, launches, and events in the period?
2. **Content pillars** — Are pillars defined? (If not, complete Content Ecosystem Planning first)
3. **Publishing capacity** — How many pieces can the team realistically produce per week/month?
4. **Channels** — Which channels does the calendar need to feed?
5. **Owner** — Who is responsible for maintaining and enforcing the calendar?

---

## Editorial Calendar Principles

### 1. Strategy Before Schedule
A calendar without a strategy is just a list of deadlines. The editorial strategy defines:
- What the business needs content to accomplish this quarter
- Which pillars and funnel stages are prioritised
- What campaign moments anchor the calendar

The calendar operationalises the strategy.

### 2. Balance Content Types
A healthy editorial calendar balances four content types:

| Type | Description | Approximate Mix |
|---|---|---|
| **Evergreen** | Timeless content; drives organic traffic long-term | 40–50% |
| **Campaign** | Content tied to specific campaign moments | 20–30% |
| **Seasonal / Timely** | Content aligned to events, trends, or category moments | 15–20% |
| **Reactive** | Unplanned responses to news, conversations, or opportunities | 10–15% |

Leave capacity for reactive content. Over-scheduled calendars cannot respond to opportunity.

### 3. Publishing Cadence Over Volume
Publishing consistently at a lower volume is more effective than publishing in bursts. Set a cadence the team can sustain at a high quality standard — then hold it.

## Process

### Step 1 — Map the Business Calendar
Before scheduling content, overlay the business calendar:
- Product launches and feature releases
- Campaigns and promotional periods
- Industry events and conferences
- Seasonal moments relevant to the category
- Internal milestones (fundraising, partnerships, awards)

Content planning happens around these moments. Anchor weeks with high business importance get content priority.

### Step 2 — Define Publishing Cadence by Channel
For each channel, set a sustainable cadence:

| Channel | Recommended Cadence |
|---|---|
| Blog / long-form | 2–4 posts per month (quality over volume) |
| Email newsletter | Weekly or bi-weekly |
| LinkedIn (organic) | 3–5 posts per week |
| YouTube | 1–2 videos per month |
| Podcast | Weekly or bi-weekly |
| SEO pillar pages | 1 per quarter per pillar |

Do not set cadences the team cannot sustain. An inconsistent schedule is worse than a slow one.

### Step 3 — Build the 90-Day Calendar
For each 4-week block, define:
- **Theme:** Which pillar or topic is prioritised this month?
- **Campaign alignment:** Which campaign does this month support?
- **Content pieces:** What specific pieces are planned?
- **Format mix:** Ensure variety — long-form, social, email, video
- **Funnel balance:** Ensure at least one piece per stage per month

Build the calendar 6–8 weeks ahead of publication. Content planned less than 2 weeks ahead is typically reactive and lower quality.

### Step 4 — Assign Ownership and Deadlines
For every content piece, define:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Title / working title | What is this piece about? |
| Pillar | Which content pillar does it belong to? |
| Funnel stage | TOFU / MOFU / BOFU / Retention |
| Format | Blog, video, email, social, etc. |
| Channel(s) | Where will it be published? |
| Primary keyword (if SEO) | What search query is it targeting? |
| Brief due | When does the brief need to be written? |
| Draft due | When is the first draft expected? |
| Review due | When must review be complete? |
| Publish date | When does it go live? |
| Owner | Who is responsible for delivery? |
| Status | Not started / In progress / In review / Scheduled / Published |

### Step 5 — Build the Review and Approval Workflow
Define the editorial process:
1. Brief created and approved
2. First draft produced
3. Editorial review (clarity, accuracy, quality)
4. SEO review (if applicable)
5. Legal or compliance review (if required)
6. Final approval
7. CMS upload and formatting
8. Distribution (social, email, paid amplification)

Each step should have an assigned reviewer and a maximum review duration. A review step with no deadline is a publishing bottleneck waiting to happen.

### Step 6 — Set the Review Cadence
- **Weekly:** Confirm next 2 weeks of content is on track; surface blockers
- **Monthly:** Review pillar and funnel balance; adjust based on performance data
- **Quarterly:** Strategic review — are we publishing what we planned? Is it performing? What changes?

## Output Formats

### Annual Editorial Calendar (Strategic)
12-month view showing:
- Monthly theme by pillar
- Key business moments and campaign periods
- Major content pieces planned per month
- Format and channel mix by quarter

### 90-Day Editorial Calendar (Operational)
Week-by-week view showing:
- Every content piece with owner, deadline, and status
- Campaign alignment
- Funnel stage distribution

### Content Brief Template
Standardised brief format including:
- Title and working description
- Audience and persona
- Pillar and funnel stage
- Primary and secondary keywords (if SEO)
- Key message and POV
- Proof points and sources
- CTA
- Format and length
- Deadline

### Workflow Template
Step-by-step production checklist from brief to publish, with owner and deadline for each step.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Calendar built without capacity assessment.** A plan that requires 10x the team's output capacity is not a plan — it is pressure.
- **No campaign alignment.** Editorial content and campaign content planned separately are half as powerful.
- **Over-scheduling.** A 100%-filled calendar leaves no room for reactive content, sudden opportunities, or production delays.
- **No funnel balance.** Calendars tend to default to TOFU content. Mandate MOFU and BOFU pieces explicitly.
- **Calendar as a to-do list, not a strategy.** The calendar should reflect strategic priorities. If every month looks identical regardless of business context, the strategy isn't driving the calendar.

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Is the business calendar mapped before content topics are assigned?
- [ ] Does each month have a theme tied to a pillar and a campaign moment?
- [ ] Is publishing cadence set to a standard the team can sustain at high quality?
- [ ] Does every planned piece have a named owner and production deadlines?
- [ ] Is there a funnel balance check — at least one MOFU and BOFU piece per month?
