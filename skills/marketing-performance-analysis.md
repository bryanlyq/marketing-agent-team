# Marketing Performance Analysis

## Purpose
Build performance reporting that connects marketing activity to revenue and pipeline, identifies trends and anomalies, and frames findings as strategic implications that drive decisions.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Monthly, quarterly, or annual performance reviews
- Board or executive reporting cycles
- Diagnosing underperformance or unexpected results
- Resetting strategy based on what the data shows

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business objective** — What decisions does this analysis need to support?
2. **Time period** — What range are we reviewing, and what's the comparison baseline?
3. **Data access** — What systems and reports are available (CRM, MAP, ad platforms, web analytics)?
4. **Audience** — Board, CMO, team? Determines depth and format.
5. **Output format** — Dashboard, narrative report, presentation?

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Establish the Measurement Framework
Define what you're measuring before pulling data:

| Layer | Metrics |
|---|---|
| Business outcomes | Revenue, pipeline generated, closed-won, retention rate |
| Marketing efficiency | CAC, ROAS, CPL, marketing-sourced % of pipeline |
| Funnel performance | MQL volume and conversion, SQL conversion, opportunity win rate |
| Channel performance | Traffic, reach, engagement, cost per outcome by channel |
| Leading indicators | Brand search volume, organic growth, email health, NPS |

### Step 2 — Pull and Validate Data
- Cross-reference numbers across systems (e.g. CRM vs MAP vs ad platform)
- Flag discrepancies before analysis — don't average over data quality problems
- Establish a clean baseline: same definitions, same attribution model, same date range

### Step 3 — Identify Trends, Anomalies, and Patterns
Ask for each metric:
- Is this trending up, down, or flat versus prior period and prior year?
- Is the change within expected range or statistically significant?
- Does this align with or contradict what other metrics show?

Use a simple traffic light framework for executive-facing outputs:
- Green: on track or ahead
- Amber: watch — early underperformance signal
- Red: off track — requires intervention

### Step 4 — Diagnose Root Causes
Do not report metrics without explaining why. For underperforming metrics:
- Was it a traffic/reach problem or a conversion problem?
- Was it campaign-specific or systemic?
- Was it driven by external factors (seasonality, competitor activity) or internal execution?

### Step 5 — Frame as Strategic Implications
Every data point must close with a so-what:

| Finding | Implication |
|---|---|
| CPL increased 40% QoQ | Channel mix is shifting toward expensive demand — review paid vs organic balance |
| SQL conversion dropped from 22% to 14% | Lead quality issue — review MQL definition or sales handoff SLA |
| Organic traffic up 28% YoY | SEO investment is compounding — increase content production budget |

### Step 6 — Recommend Next Actions
Define 3–5 specific next actions, each with:
- What to do
- Who owns it
- By when
- What metric it is expected to move

---

## Output Formats

### Executive Performance Dashboard
- Period-over-period scorecard (revenue, pipeline, CAC, key channel metrics)
- Traffic light status per strategic objective
- Top 3 insights and recommended actions

### Full Performance Report
- Executive summary (5 bullets max — lead with the so-whats)
- Revenue and pipeline analysis
- Funnel conversion analysis
- Channel performance breakdown
- Budget efficiency review
- Strategic implications and next steps

### QBR Presentation
- Prior quarter review (results vs targets)
- Diagnosis of variances
- Next quarter plan adjustments
- Resource or budget reallocation recommendations

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Reporting metrics without implications.** Numbers without meaning are noise.
- **Cherry-picking favourable metrics.** Report the full picture, including what isn't working.
- **Inconsistent attribution.** Changing the model mid-analysis distorts comparisons.
- **Ignoring leading indicators.** Lagging metrics tell you what happened; leading metrics tell you what's coming.
- **Confusing causation with correlation.** A metric that moved doesn't mean your activity caused it.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Does every metric connect to a business outcome?
- [ ] Are variances explained, not just reported?
- [ ] Have I surfaced the bad news as clearly as the good news?
- [ ] Are next steps specific, owned, and tied to measurable impact?
- [ ] Would a decision-maker read this and know what to do next?
