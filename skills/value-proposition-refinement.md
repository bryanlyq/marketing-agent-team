# Value Proposition Refinement

## Purpose
Stress-test, sharpen, and strengthen the core value proposition — so it is true, relevant, and differentiated enough to drive preference and conversion.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Low conversion rates despite high traffic or leads
- Messaging that feels "correct" but isn't resonating
- Repositioning after a product change or competitive shift
- Entering a new segment with a new audience
- Preparing for a fundraise or strategic pitch

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Current proposition** — What is the existing value proposition? Where is it used?
2. **Audience** — Who is the proposition meant to convince?
3. **Problem being solved** — What job is the customer hiring this product to do?
4. **Competitive alternatives** — What would the customer do instead?
5. **Success signal** — How will we know if the refined proposition is working?

---

## The Three Tests of a Strong Value Proposition

Every value proposition must pass all three:

| Test | Question | Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|
| **True** | Can we back this up with evidence? | Credibility collapse when scrutinised |
| **Relevant** | Does this matter to the customer right now? | Ignored because it doesn't solve an active need |
| **Differentiated** | Could a competitor say the same thing? | Commoditisation; no reason to choose you |

If the proposition fails any test, it needs refinement — not just better copy.

## Frameworks

### Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)
Focus on the progress the customer is trying to make, not the product's features.

> "People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."
> — Theodore Levitt

For each target segment, define:
- **Functional job** — What task are they trying to complete?
- **Emotional job** — How do they want to feel while doing it or after?
- **Social job** — How do they want to be perceived by others as a result?

The strongest value propositions address all three levels, even if implicitly.

### Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder)
Map the fit between:

**Customer Profile:**
- Customer jobs (what they're trying to do)
- Pains (what gets in the way, what they fear)
- Gains (desired outcomes and benefits)

**Value Map:**
- Pain relievers (how you alleviate pains)
- Gain creators (how you deliver desired outcomes)
- Products/services (what you offer)

Fit = your gain creators and pain relievers directly address the customer's most important gains and pains.

### Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)
Define value in terms of desired outcomes, not features:
- What metric does the customer want to improve?
- How important is that outcome?
- How satisfied are they with current solutions?

High importance + low satisfaction = underserved outcome = value proposition opportunity.

## Process

### Step 1 — Audit the Current Proposition
Rate the existing proposition against the three tests (True / Relevant / Differentiated):
- What evidence supports each claim?
- Is this what customers say they need, or what the business thinks they need?
- Do competitors make the same claim?

### Step 2 — Return to Customer Language
Pull verbatims from:
- Customer interviews and discovery calls
- NPS qualitative responses
- Review sites (G2, Trustpilot, Capterra)
- Sales call recordings
- Support tickets (what problems are people articulating?)

Identify the exact language customers use to describe:
- The problem before they found you
- The outcome they experienced after
- Why they chose you over alternatives

### Step 3 — Map Against Alternatives
For each claim in the proposition, ask:
- What would the customer do instead?
- How does this claim compare against that alternative?
- Is the differentiation meaningful to the customer or just internally significant?

### Step 4 — Draft Refined Propositions
Write 3–5 variations, each leading with a different job, pain, or outcome.

Use this structure as a starting point:
> **For** [audience], **who** [have this problem], **[brand/product]** is a [category] **that** [primary benefit]. **Unlike** [alternative], **we** [key differentiator].

Then simplify ruthlessly. Most propositions are 60% longer than they need to be.

### Step 5 — Test and Validate
Where possible, test variations:
- Qualitative: concept testing with target customers — do they recognise themselves?
- Quantitative: A/B test headlines on landing pages, ads, or email subject lines
- Sales: does the refined proposition reduce objections or shorten deal cycles?

Beware internal preference. What the team loves is often not what the customer responds to.

---

## Output Formats

### Value Proposition Canvas
Completed canvas showing customer profile vs value map with explicit fit articulation.

### Proposition Comparison Matrix
| Version | Audience | Lead Job | Key Claim | Differentiation | Proof Point |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

### Refined Proposition Statement
Clean, tested statement with:
- Primary version (2–3 sentences)
- Elevator version (1 sentence)
- Headline version (6–10 words)

### Testing Brief
For A/B or concept testing:
- Hypothesis: which version will outperform and why?
- Test design: where, sample size, duration, success metric
- Decision criteria: what result triggers adoption of the new proposition?

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Feature-led propositions.** Features are proof. Benefits are the proposition. Lead with the outcome.
- **Propositions written by the product team.** Internal knowledge creates messaging blind spots. Validate with customers.
- **Untested propositions.** Even a simple 5-person concept test reveals gaps that internal review misses.
- **Propositions that mean different things to different audiences.** If the proposition needs interpretation, it needs rewriting.
- **Ignoring the alternative.** A proposition only has value relative to what the customer would otherwise do.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Does the proposition pass all three tests: True, Relevant, Differentiated?
- [ ] Is it written in customer language, not internal language?
- [ ] Has it been validated against actual customer verbatims?
- [ ] Is the differentiation from alternatives — not just competitors?
- [ ] Is there a testing plan to validate the refined proposition in market?
