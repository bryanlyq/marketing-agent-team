# Crisis Communication Planning

## Purpose
Build pre-crisis response playbooks for likely risk scenarios, define escalation protocols, spokesperson roles, and approval processes, and design post-crisis reputation recovery plans.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Building or auditing crisis communication preparedness
- Before a major product launch, IPO, or high-profile campaign (when exposure increases)
- After an incident that exposed gaps in the current response process
- Annual operational risk review

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Business context** — Industry, size, public profile, and regulatory environment?
2. **Risk landscape** — What crisis scenarios are plausible given the business model and audience?
3. **Stakeholder map** — Who must be communicated with in a crisis (customers, investors, media, employees)?
4. **Current capability** — What crisis protocols, if any, already exist?
5. **Output format** — Crisis playbook, scenario templates, or governance framework?

---

## Process

### Step 1 — Identify Likely Crisis Scenarios
Conduct a risk scenario mapping exercise. Common categories:

| Category | Example Scenarios |
|---|---|
| Product / service failure | Outage, data breach, product defect, harmful content |
| Customer harm | Injury, financial loss, data exposure, discriminatory outcome |
| Reputational attack | False claims, activist targeting, executive misconduct allegation |
| Regulatory / legal | Investigation, fine, legal action, regulatory non-compliance |
| Operational | Supply chain failure, partner misconduct, third-party breach |
| Cultural / social | Controversy over company statement, brand association with negative event |
| Market | Hostile M&A, activist investor, short seller report |

For each scenario, assess:
- **Likelihood:** How probable is this in the next 12 months?
- **Impact:** How severe would the reputational and commercial damage be?
- **Preparedness:** Is there a response plan for this today?

Prioritise high-likelihood and high-impact scenarios for full playbook development.

### Step 2 — Define the Crisis Response Team
Establish roles before a crisis occurs:

| Role | Responsibility | Named Individual |
|---|---|---|
| Crisis Lead | Coordinates overall response; primary decision-maker | — |
| Communications Lead | Drafts and approves all external messaging | — |
| Legal Counsel | Reviews all statements for legal risk | — |
| Executive Spokesperson | Faces media and public; briefed by comms lead | — |
| Operations Lead | Manages internal response and remediation | — |
| Social Media Monitor | Tracks real-time developments and sentiment | — |

### Step 3 — Build the Escalation Protocol
Define when something becomes a crisis and who is notified:

**Triage levels:**

| Level | Definition | Escalation | Decision Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 — Watch | Early signal, contained issue | Marketing aware | Marketing lead |
| Tier 2 — Active | Media pickup or viral social content | CMO + Legal briefed | CMO |
| Tier 3 — Crisis | National media, regulatory involvement, or significant customer harm | Full crisis team activated | CEO + Legal |

**Escalation trigger:** Any team member can escalate. The cost of unnecessary escalation is low; the cost of missed escalation is high.

### Step 4 — Build Scenario Playbooks
For each priority scenario, document:

**Holding statement:** A pre-approved first response that can be issued within 1 hour while the full response is being developed.

Example holding statement structure:
> "We are aware of [issue]. The safety/interests of our [customers/employees/partners] is our priority. We are investigating urgently and will provide an update within [timeframe]. [Contact info for affected parties]."

**Response sequence:**
1. Internal notification (who hears about this first, and how?)
2. Fact-finding (what actually happened — get the truth before responding publicly)
3. Initial public statement (holding statement within 1 hour of decision to respond)
4. Stakeholder communication (employees, partners, investors in parallel with or before public)
5. Full public response (within 4–24 hours depending on severity)
6. Ongoing updates (frequency and format)
7. Resolution statement
8. Post-crisis review

**Channel-specific protocols:**
- Social media: Pause scheduled content; respond to direct queries; do not delete negative comments unless illegal
- Media: Designated spokesperson only; other employees refer all enquiries to comms team
- Employees: Internal communication before or simultaneous with public statement
- Investors / board: Brief via direct call, not public statement first

### Step 5 — Prepare Response Templates
For each priority scenario, pre-draft:
- Holding statement
- Full public statement (multiple versions for varying severity)
- Customer email
- Employee internal communication
- Social media post
- Press statement / media enquiry response

All templates should be reviewed and legally approved before they are needed.

### Step 6 — Design the Post-Crisis Recovery Plan
A crisis is not over when the immediate incident is resolved:

**Short-term (0–4 weeks):**
- Monitor sentiment to assess ongoing reputational impact
- Follow up with directly affected stakeholders
- Internal debrief: what happened and how could it have been prevented?
- Review and update crisis playbook based on lessons learned

**Medium-term (1–6 months):**
- Proactive reputation rebuilding: earned media, customer stories, transparency reports
- Demonstrate that the root cause has been addressed
- Rebuild trust with most-affected stakeholders through direct engagement

**Long-term (6+ months):**
- Brand health tracking to measure recovery trajectory
- Sustained narrative investment to reframe brand associations
- Structural changes to prevent recurrence

## Output Formats

### Crisis Communication Playbook
- Risk scenario register with prioritisation
- Crisis response team structure and contacts
- Escalation protocol with triage levels
- Scenario-specific playbooks (per priority risk)
- Pre-approved response templates
- Post-crisis recovery framework

### Scenario Response Template
- Scenario description
- Escalation trigger
- Holding statement (pre-approved)
- Response sequence with timing
- Channel-specific actions
- Spokesperson briefing notes

### Post-Crisis Review Report
- Timeline of events
- Response assessment (what worked, what didn't)
- Stakeholder impact analysis
- Playbook updates required
- Structural changes recommended

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Building the playbook during the crisis.** Improvised responses under pressure are slower, inconsistent, and error-prone.
- **Delaying response while perfecting the statement.** A holding statement within one hour is worth more than a polished statement in six.
- **Communicating externally before employees.** Employees who hear about a crisis from news or social media before management lose trust quickly.
- **Deleting negative social content.** Deletion escalates rather than resolves. Engage or leave unless the content is illegal.
- **No post-crisis recovery plan.** Without deliberate recovery effort, the incident defines the brand for longer than necessary.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Are the highest-probability, highest-impact scenarios prioritised for full playbook development?
- [ ] Is the crisis response team named with clear role definitions?
- [ ] Is the escalation protocol defined with triage levels and triggers?
- [ ] Are pre-approved holding statements ready for each priority scenario?
- [ ] Is there a post-crisis recovery plan, not just an incident response plan?
