# Sales and Marketing Alignment

## Purpose
Build shared definitions, processes, and feedback loops so sales and marketing operate as a unified revenue function — not competing departments with misaligned incentives.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- High MQL volume but low SQL conversion — "marketing sends rubbish leads"
- Pipeline shortfalls blamed on the wrong team
- No shared definition of a qualified lead
- Sales team not using marketing content or tools
- Poor follow-up SLA or lead routing breakdown
- Go-to-market planning for a new product or segment

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Current friction points** — Where does the handoff break down? What's the root complaint on each side?
2. **Existing definitions** — Are there documented MQL/SQL definitions? Are they used?
3. **Data access** — What CRM and MAP data is available to diagnose the gap?
4. **Stakeholders** — Who from sales and marketing needs to be aligned (CMO + CRO, or team leads)?
5. **Objective** — Is this a process fix, a metrics alignment, or a structural change?

---

## The Core Alignment Problems

| Symptom | Root Cause |
|---|---|
| "Leads are low quality" | No shared ICP definition; scoring miscalibrated |
| "Sales doesn't follow up" | SLA not defined or not enforced |
| "Marketing doesn't understand what we need" | No feedback loop from sales to marketing |
| "We can't measure marketing's contribution" | Attribution model not agreed or not in place |
| "We're targeting different audiences" | Separate GTM plans never reconciled |
| "Sales ignores our content" | Content built for marketing's audience, not buyers' questions |

Alignment is a systems problem, not a personality problem. Fix the process, not the people.

---

## Framework: The Revenue Alignment Model

Five components must be aligned for sales and marketing to operate as one function:

```
1. SHARED DEFINITIONS (MQL, SQL, ICP, opportunity)
2. SHARED METRICS (pipeline, conversion rates, revenue)
3. SHARED PROCESS (lead routing, handoff SLAs, feedback loops)
4. SHARED CONTENT (assets that work for buyers at each stage)
5. SHARED RHYTHM (joint planning, pipeline reviews, retrospectives)
```

## Process

### Step 1 — Audit the Current Handoff
Map the current lead journey from MQL to closed-won:
- How is a lead generated and captured?
- How is it scored and qualified as an MQL?
- How is it routed to sales?
- What is the follow-up SLA and is it being met?
- What happens to leads that sales doesn't pursue?
- What happens to leads that don't convert?

Calculate:
- MQL → SAL conversion rate (sales accepted leads)
- SAL → SQL conversion rate
- SQL → opportunity rate
- Time from MQL to first sales contact

Any SAL rate below 70% signals a definition problem. Any significant SLA breach signals a process or incentive problem.

### Step 2 — Define Shared Lead Definitions

**Marketing Qualified Lead (MQL)**
A lead that has demonstrated enough fit and intent, based on the agreed scoring model, to be worth sales attention.
- Criteria: ICP fit score + engagement score ≥ threshold
- Owner: Marketing — responsible for delivering MQLs at target volume and quality

**Sales Accepted Lead (SAL)**
An MQL that sales has reviewed and agreed is worth pursuing.
- Criteria: Sales reviews within SLA and confirms it meets qualification criteria
- Owner: Sales — if rejecting, must specify reason in CRM

**Sales Qualified Lead (SQL)**
A lead that sales has engaged with and confirmed has BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing) or equivalent qualification.
- Criteria: Defined qualification framework (MEDDIC, BANT, SPICED)
- Owner: Sales — responsible for moving SQLs to opportunities at target rate

**Why this matters:** If marketing's MQL is sales' disqualified lead, both teams are measuring success differently. The definitions close the gap.

### Step 3 — Build the Lead Scoring Model Together
Involve sales in defining:
- Which demographic/firmographic signals indicate fit
- Which behavioural signals indicate intent
- What the MQL threshold should be (calibrated to conversion rates)
- What constitutes a disqualifying signal

A scoring model that sales doesn't trust will be ignored. Co-creation drives adoption.

### Step 4 — Define SLAs and Routing Rules
Document and agree:
- Time-to-follow-up by lead source and tier (e.g., demo requests: 1 hour; content downloads: 24 hours)
- Lead routing logic (by geography, segment, AE assignment, round-robin)
- What happens to aged MQLs that sales hasn't actioned
- Consequences of SLA breach (escalation path, reporting)

### Step 5 — Build the Feedback Loop
Marketing needs signal from sales to improve lead quality. Sales needs signal from marketing to understand buyer context.

Structure:
- **Weekly:** Sales flags poor-quality leads in CRM with a reason code (bad fit, bad timing, duplicate, not a decision-maker)
- **Monthly:** Marketing reviews lead quality feedback and adjusts scoring, channel mix, or targeting
- **Quarterly:** Joint review of MQL → revenue conversion rates; recalibrate definitions if drift is significant

### Step 6 — Align on Content and Enablement
Audit whether sales is using marketing content:
- Which assets are being used, and in what context?
- Which are never used, and why?
- What content do sellers create themselves because nothing suitable exists?

Build a content bridge: for each stage of the sales cycle, ensure there is a relevant asset — and that sales knows it exists and how to use it.

### Step 7 — Establish a Shared Rhythm
Build a regular meeting cadence:
- **Weekly pipeline review** — Marketing visibility into pipeline health; identify top-of-funnel gaps
- **Monthly GTM sync** — Review lead quality, content gaps, competitive intelligence, upcoming campaigns
- **Quarterly planning session** — Joint target-setting, ICP review, channel strategy, content roadmap

## Output Formats

### Alignment Framework Document
- Shared definitions (MQL, SAL, SQL, ICP)
- Lead scoring model
- SLA and routing rules
- Feedback loop process
- Escalation path

### SLA Document
| Lead Type | Follow-Up SLA | Routing Rule | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demo request | 1 business hour | Direct to AE by territory | Sales |
| Pricing page visit + form | 4 business hours | SDR round-robin | Sales Development |
| Content download (MQL threshold met) | 24 business hours | SDR queue | Sales Development |

### Lead Quality Feedback Template (CRM)
Reason codes for sales rejection:
- Not ICP fit (company size, industry, geography)
- Not a decision-maker or influencer
- No active need or initiative
- Competitor: already using [X]
- Bad timing: revisit in [date]
- Duplicate or existing account

### Shared KPI Dashboard
- MQL volume (by source)
- MQL → SAL conversion rate
- SAL → SQL conversion rate
- SQL → opportunity rate
- Time from MQL to first contact
- Pipeline sourced by marketing
- Revenue influenced by marketing

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Alignment as a one-time workshop.** Alignment decays. It requires a sustained operating rhythm.
- **Definitions agreed in a meeting but never enforced in the CRM.** Definitions must be operationalised in the system of record.
- **Feedback loop that flows one way.** Marketing needs signal from sales; sales needs context from marketing. Both directions matter.
- **Content alignment skipped.** If sales can't find or won't use marketing content, buyers won't see it.
- **Shared metrics that aren't truly shared.** If marketing is measured on MQL volume and sales on revenue, the incentives are misaligned regardless of what was agreed.

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Are MQL, SAL, and SQL definitions documented, agreed, and operationalised in the CRM?
- [ ] Is there a lead scoring model that sales helped define?
- [ ] Are SLAs and routing rules documented with clear ownership?
- [ ] Is there a structured feedback loop from sales back to marketing?
- [ ] Is there a regular joint cadence (weekly pipeline, monthly sync, quarterly planning)?
