# Customer Insight Analysis

## Purpose
Synthesise qualitative and quantitative customer data into strategic insight that changes how the business thinks about its audience, its messaging, or its product — not just what it knows.

---

## When to Use This Skill
- Messaging isn't resonating and the reason isn't clear
- Segmentation or ICP needs to be validated or refined
- High churn with no obvious cause
- Preparing for a product launch or repositioning
- Building a business case for a new market or segment
- Designing a campaign that needs to speak in the customer's language

---

## Briefing Checklist
Before starting, confirm:
1. **Decision to inform** — What strategic decision does this insight need to support?
2. **Data available** — What customer data already exists? What gaps need to be filled?
3. **Audience** — Which segment or customer group are we analysing?
4. **Timeframe** — Is this a rapid analysis or a structured research programme?
5. **Output audience** — Who will use this insight and in what format?

---

## Insight vs Information

The most common failure in customer analysis is stopping at information.

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| **Data** | "42% of customers rated us 7/10 on ease of use" |
| **Information** | "Ease of use ratings are lower among customers who joined in the last 6 months" |
| **Insight** | "New customers struggle because our onboarding skips the configuration step that experienced users handle manually — there's a hidden competency gap we've never addressed" |
| **Implication** | "Redesigning onboarding to surface the configuration step could reduce early churn by 15–20% and improve 90-day NPS" |

Insight is the moment of understanding *why* — not just *what*. Implication is the moment of knowing *what to do about it*.

## Data Sources

### Qualitative Sources
| Source | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| Customer interviews | Motivations, language, decision logic, unmet needs |
| Sales call recordings | Objections, buying triggers, competitor comparisons |
| Support tickets | Friction, misalignment between expectation and reality |
| NPS verbatims | Delight drivers and churn drivers in the customer's own words |
| Churn interviews | Real reasons for leaving (not the polite CRM reasons) |
| Social listening | Unsolicited opinions, category language, competitor conversations |
| Review sites (G2, Capterra) | Patterns at scale; language customers use publicly |

### Quantitative Sources
| Source | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| NPS / CSAT surveys | Satisfaction trends and segment differences |
| Product usage data | Adoption patterns, feature engagement, activation rates |
| CRM cohort data | Retention, expansion, deal size by segment |
| Email engagement | Content relevance by topic and segment |
| Web analytics | Content interest, funnel behaviour, high-exit pages |
| Win/loss data | Competitive displacement patterns and reasons |

## Process

### Step 1 — Define the Research Questions
Before gathering data, define 3–5 specific questions that, if answered, would change a decision:
- "Why do customers in the [X] segment churn 3x faster than the overall base?"
- "What job do buyers hire our product to do — and how does that differ from our assumed use case?"
- "Why do customers who come via referral have 40% better retention?"

Vague inputs produce vague insights. Specificity in the research question forces precision in the analysis.

### Step 2 — Gather and Organise Data
Collect data from 2–3 sources minimum. Single-source analysis is always fragile.

For qualitative data:
- Interview minimum 5–8 customers per segment (patterns emerge quickly with good sampling)
- Use open-ended questions; avoid leading questions
- Record and transcribe (with permission); analyse verbatims, not paraphrases

For quantitative data:
- Segment before analysing; averages hide the real story
- Compare cohorts: new vs mature customers, segment A vs B, churned vs retained
- Look for correlations, but validate causation qualitatively

### Step 3 — Identify Patterns
Across all data sources, look for:
- **Themes that repeat** across different sources and segments
- **Contradictions** — where different sources tell a different story (always investigate)
- **Surprises** — findings that contradict existing assumptions (highest-value insights)
- **Absence** — things that customers don't say may matter as much as what they do say

### Step 4 — Build the Insight Statement

For each finding, write the insight using this structure:
> **Finding:** [What the data shows]
> **Why it matters:** [What it means strategically]
> **Implication:** [What the business should do differently]

This structure forces you to move from observation to recommendation. If you can't complete the third line, it's not yet a useful insight — it's still just a finding.

### Step 5 — Validate and Pressure-Test
Before presenting insights:
- Is this finding consistent across multiple sources or isolated to one?
- Could this be explained by sample bias or data quality issues?
- Does this insight contradict a widely held internal belief? If so, is the evidence strong enough?
- Have we stress-tested the implication — could it have unintended consequences?

### Step 6 — Frame for Action
Present insights in order of strategic importance, not data volume.

Every insight document should end with a clear list of recommended next steps:
- What should the business do differently?
- Who should own each action?
- What would validate or challenge this insight further?

## Research Methods: Quick Reference

### Customer Interviews (VOC)
**When to use:** Defining messaging, validating ICP, understanding churn, preparing for repositioning
**How to conduct:**
- 45–60 minute structured conversation
- Recruit from: churned customers, power users, recent converts, at-risk accounts
- Key questions: "Walk me through the moment you decided you needed a solution like this." "What would you tell a colleague who was considering us?"
- Analyse: categorise verbatims; count theme frequency; surface contradictions

### NPS Diagnostic
**When to use:** Understanding why satisfaction is high or low; prioritising improvements
**How to conduct:**
- Score-only NPS reveals nothing. Always pair with open-text "why"
- Segment NPS by cohort, segment, channel, tenure
- Analyse verbatim themes for promoters vs detractors separately
- Do not average verbatims — read them

### Exit Interviews (Churn Research)
**When to use:** Diagnosing churn causes at the root, not the surface
**How to conduct:**
- Within 2 weeks of churn notice
- Ask: "What was the main reason you decided to leave?" then probe one level deeper
- Do not accept "price" as a final answer — price is rarely the real reason
- Compare exit interview themes to CRM loss reason codes — they rarely match

### Survey Research
**When to use:** Validating qualitative findings at scale; segmenting attitudes
**Best practices:**
- Keep surveys under 10 minutes
- Ask one thing per question
- Use scales consistently
- Pilot on 5 people before sending broadly

## Output Formats

### Insight Report
- Research questions and methodology
- Executive summary: top 3–5 insights and their implications
- Detailed findings by theme (with verbatims and data points)
- Strategic implications section
- Recommended actions

### Strategic Implications Document
For each insight:
- Insight statement
- Evidence (quantitative and qualitative)
- Strategic implication
- Recommended action
- Owner and timeline

### Research Brief (for commissioning new research)
- Business decision to inform
- Research questions
- Recommended methodology
- Sample definition
- Timeline and deliverables

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Stopping at the finding.** Data and information are not insight. Push to the implication.
- **Averaging qualitative data.** Themes don't average. Read the verbatims.
- **Asking leading questions.** "You find it difficult to use, right?" produces confirmation, not insight.
- **Ignoring contradictory data.** Findings that contradict assumptions are the most valuable. Don't explain them away.
- **Insight without action.** A beautiful insight document that changes no decision was an expensive waste of time.

---

## Quality Check
Before submitting, ask:
- [ ] Does every insight have a finding, a "why it matters," and an implication?
- [ ] Is the insight grounded in data from at least two independent sources?
- [ ] Have contradictions and surprises been investigated, not glossed over?
- [ ] Is the insight written in language a decision-maker can act on?
- [ ] Does the document close with specific, owned next steps?
